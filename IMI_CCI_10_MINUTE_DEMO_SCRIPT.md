# IMI CCI-Style 10-Minute Demo Script

Use case: Severe-service control valve management demo  
Positioning: Engineering Proposal Intelligence for Severe-Service Control Valve Companies  
Important: Do not use IMI CCI logo, trademarks, or partnership language. Refer to this as an IMI CCI-style severe-service control valve management demo.

---

## 0:00-1:00 Executive Opening

Presenter action: Open WinsProposal dashboard or demo landing workspace.

Say:  
“Thank you for the time today. I’ll keep this focused and practical. This is a severe-service control valve management demo showing how WinsProposal can help proposal and application engineering teams turn complex RFPs into more complete, compliant, and management-visible proposals. The intent is not to replace engineering judgment or internal sizing tools. The intent is to reduce manual proposal effort, preserve senior engineering knowledge, improve compliance coverage, and make proposal readiness visible earlier.”

Click: Open the demo opportunity labeled “Severe-Service Control Valve RFP.”

Say:  
“I’ll walk through one representative RFP flow in ten minutes, then we can discuss whether a 30-day pilot is worth exploring.”

---

## 1:00-2:00 Severe-Service RFP Upload / RFP Context

Presenter action: Navigate to RFP upload or proposal workspace.

Click: Select or show uploaded RFP package.

Say:  
“The workflow starts with the RFP package. In a typical severe-service bid, the requirements are not in one clean document. They may be spread across the main enquiry, datasheets, valve schedules, standards, exceptions, customer forms, and technical bid evaluation formats.”

Click: Open the RFP context panel.

Say:  
“WinsProposal creates a structured workspace around the opportunity: customer context, service type, valve subtype, bid timeline, technical documents, and proposal status. This gives the proposal team a shared starting point instead of working from scattered files and email threads.”

---

## 2:00-3:00 Requirement Extraction

Presenter action: Open requirement extraction screen.

Click: Run or show extracted requirements.

Say:  
“Next, the system extracts requirements from the RFP package. The objective is to move from manual reading to structured review. Here we can see technical requirements, commercial requirements, compliance items, exclusions, documentation needs, and customer-specific response expectations.”

Click: Filter to technical requirements.

Say:  
“For severe-service valves, the important part is not just keyword extraction. The proposal team needs to know where the bid may involve cavitation, flashing, noise, high pressure drop, erosion, material risk, leakage class, actuator requirements, or special testing.”

Click: Open requirement source reference.

Say:  
“Each item should remain traceable to the source document so the engineer or proposal manager can verify it before submission.”

---

## 3:00-4:00 Knowledge Vault Reuse

Presenter action: Open Knowledge Vault.

Click: Show reusable sections matched to severe-service context.

Say:  
“This is the Knowledge Vault. The idea is to preserve senior engineering and proposal knowledge in a structured way. Instead of searching old folders or asking the same senior engineer for similar language again, the team can reuse approved content by valve type, application, service risk, customer requirement, or standard.”

Click: Open a reusable technical note or approved proposal section.

Say:  
“For example, if the RFP raises cavitation or noise concerns, the system can surface previously approved language, assumptions, clarification questions, and proposal notes. This still requires review, but it reduces the blank-page work and protects institutional knowledge.”

---

## 4:00-5:30 Engineering Intelligence Section

Presenter action: Open engineering intelligence panel.

Click: Show extracted process conditions and severe-service risk flags.

Say:  
“Now we move into proposal-stage engineering intelligence. This is not a replacement for sizing or final engineering. It is a way to summarize what the RFP appears to require and identify what needs technical attention.”

Click: Show risk flags.

Say:  
“Here the system highlights severe-service considerations such as pressure drop, cavitation or flashing potential, noise limits, material compatibility, leakage class, actuator requirements, testing requirements, and documentation obligations.”

Click: Show assumptions and clarifications.

Say:  
“The useful output for the team is a structured list of assumptions, risks, and clarification questions. This helps application engineering focus on the highest-value review points instead of spending time reconstructing the RFP from scratch.”

Click: Show proposal recommendation or engineering summary.

Say:  
“For management, this also creates an early view of technical risk before the bid reaches final approval.”

---

## 5:30-6:30 Compliance/TBE Workflow

Presenter action: Open compliance or TBE matrix.

Click: Show compliance coverage dashboard.

Say:  
“Compliance and TBE effort is often one of the most time-consuming parts of a technical proposal. WinsProposal converts requirements into a response matrix with ownership, status, source reference, response notes, and risk level.”

Click: Filter to open items or exceptions.

Say:  
“The proposal lead can quickly see what is complete, what needs engineering input, what needs a commercial response, and what may need an exception or clarification.”

Click: Open one TBE item.

Say:  
“This is especially useful when customer scoring depends on complete technical bid evaluation responses. The goal is to reduce late-stage discovery of missing compliance items.”

---

## 6:30-7:30 Technical Visuals/Drawings

Presenter action: Open visuals or drawings section.

Click: Show generated technical visual.

Say:  
“Technical visuals help management and customers understand the proposal more quickly. WinsProposal can assist with proposal-ready diagrams such as application context, response structure, compliance flow, or technical explanation visuals.”

Click: Show edit or review state.

Say:  
“These visuals are not treated as final engineering drawings without review. They are communication assets that engineering can approve, edit, or replace as needed.”

Click: Show visual inserted into proposal preview.

Say:  
“The value is consistency and speed. Proposal teams spend less time rebuilding simple visuals and more time on the actual technical response.”

---

## 7:30-8:30 Executive KPI Dashboard

Presenter action: Open executive dashboard.

Click: Show KPI cards.

Say:  
“For senior management, this is the operating view. The dashboard shows proposal status, engineering bottlenecks, compliance coverage, TBE progress, approval cycle time, and throughput.”

Click: Show opportunity risk or aging approvals.

Say:  
“Instead of waiting until the final review meeting to discover gaps, management can see which bids are at risk, where approvals are delayed, and where engineering capacity is being consumed.”

Click: Show pilot metrics.

Say:  
“In a pilot, these same metrics become the basis for ROI: turnaround reduction, engineering hours saved, reuse percentage, compliance coverage, and approval cycle improvement.”

---

## 8:30-9:30 PDF/DOCX Export

Presenter action: Open proposal preview and export controls.

Click: Preview proposal document.

Say:  
“Once the proposal content is reviewed, the team can generate a structured proposal document. The export is intended to support PDF or DOCX-ready management and customer-facing formats.”

Click: Show sections in exported proposal.

Say:  
“The output can include executive summary, scope understanding, technical response, compliance matrix, TBE responses, assumptions, deviations, clarifications, and supporting visuals.”

Click: Show export option.

Say:  
“The important point is that the exported proposal is backed by structured requirements, reused knowledge, engineering review, and compliance tracking rather than being assembled manually at the end.”

---

## 9:30-10:00 Pilot Proposal and Ask

Presenter action: Open pilot slide or scorecard.

Say:  
“The recommended next step is not a large implementation. It is a focused 30-day pilot using two or three representative severe-service valve RFPs. We would define baseline metrics, configure approved knowledge content, run the workflow, and review measurable outcomes with management.”

Click: Show pilot success metrics.

Say:  
“If the pilot shows faster turnaround, reduced engineering effort, stronger compliance coverage, and better executive visibility, then we can discuss the right operating model. Would it make sense to identify one proposal owner, one engineering owner, and one compliance owner for a pilot scoping discussion?”
