# IMI CCI-Style Objection Handling

Use these responses in senior management conversations for a severe-service control valve proposal intelligence demo. Keep the tone calm, practical, and respectful of existing engineering maturity.

---

## “We already have proposal templates.”

Response:  
“That is a strength, and WinsProposal is not meant to replace approved templates. Templates usually define structure, but they do not always extract RFP requirements, identify missing compliance items, reuse historical engineering knowledge by context, or show management where the proposal is blocked. The pilot can use your existing templates as the output format while improving the intelligence behind them.”

Follow-up question:  
“Where do templates stop helping today: RFP interpretation, engineering inputs, compliance tracking, or approvals?”

---

## “Our engineering is too specialized.”

Response:  
“That is exactly why the workflow should preserve and organize your engineering knowledge rather than treat every proposal as generic. Severe-service control valve proposals depend on experienced judgment. WinsProposal is designed to surface relevant prior knowledge, flag proposal-stage risks, and prepare structured review items so specialized engineers can focus on the highest-value decisions.”

Follow-up question:  
“Which recurring engineering topics consume the most senior engineer time during proposal preparation?”

---

## “AI may produce inaccurate technical outputs.”

Response:  
“That concern is valid. The right operating model is AI-assisted, engineering-approved. WinsProposal should not be the final technical authority for sizing, selection, or commitments. It should extract, summarize, flag, and draft reviewable content with source references and approval status. Technical outputs should remain subject to engineering validation before customer submission.”

Follow-up question:  
“Which outputs would you allow AI to draft, and which outputs must always require explicit engineering approval?”

---

## “We use internal sizing tools.”

Response:  
“WinsProposal is not intended to replace internal sizing tools. Those tools remain the authority for sizing and selection. WinsProposal sits around the proposal workflow: RFP interpretation, requirement extraction, knowledge reuse, compliance/TBE tracking, assumptions, clarifications, visuals, and export. It can complement sizing tools by improving the quality and traceability of the proposal package.”

Follow-up question:  
“Where does the output from the sizing tool currently enter the proposal document, and how much manual work is needed around it?”

---

## “Data security is a concern.”

Response:  
“That should be addressed before any pilot. A pilot can be scoped with approved sample RFPs, anonymized documents, limited users, controlled uploads, and clear rules on what content enters the Knowledge Vault. The conversation should include access control, data retention, document sensitivity, and approval boundaries before live customer data is used.”

Follow-up question:  
“What data restrictions would need to be satisfied for a limited pilot using representative documents?”

---

## “We are not ready for another system.”

Response:  
“The pilot does not need to become another enterprise system. It can be run as a focused workflow around a few representative RFPs to test whether the value is real. If the team does not see measurable improvement in turnaround, engineering effort, compliance coverage, or visibility, there is no reason to expand it.”

Follow-up question:  
“Would a lightweight 30-day pilot be acceptable if it uses limited users and existing proposal documents?”

---

## “We need integration with existing tools.”

Response:  
“Integration may matter for scale, but it does not need to block a pilot. The first step is to prove workflow value with representative RFPs and outputs. Once the business case is clear, integrations can be prioritized around the systems that matter most, such as document repositories, CRM, ERP, sizing tools, approval workflows, or export formats.”

Follow-up question:  
“Which integration would create the highest operational value first?”

---

## “Management needs ROI first.”

Response:  
“Agreed. That is why the recommended approach is a scorecard-driven pilot. We can establish baseline metrics for turnaround time, engineering hours, reuse percentage, compliance coverage, TBE completion time, approval cycle time, throughput, and executive visibility. The pilot should be judged on measurable improvement, not general enthusiasm.”

Follow-up question:  
“Which three metrics would management trust most when deciding whether to continue?”
